## CATEGORY 2: FRAMEWORKS & METHODOLOGY (15 POSTS)

### POST 11: THE POP FRAMEWORK

Every AI platform. One framework.

Whether you're using ChatGPT, Claude, your HRIS chatbot, or a custom tool—this works:

**P - Persona:** Who should AI act as?
**O - Objective:** What specific task should it accomplish?
**P - Parameters:** How should it deliver?

Example:

BAD PROMPT:
"Write me a job description for an HR manager"

GOOD PROMPT:
"You are an experienced talent acquisition specialist [P]. Create a job description for a Senior HRBP in a 200-person tech company [O]. Use inclusive language, focus on outcomes rather than years of experience, keep it under 500 words, ensure EEOC compliance [P]."

Same AI. Same prompt box.

Completely different results.

### POST 12: THE FULCRUM METHODOLOGY

Think of AI as a lever.

You're the fulcrum—the point of balance and control.

Without the fulcrum, the lever does nothing.

Without your judgment, AI outputs are just faux work product—looks finished but isn't actually what needs to be done.

This is why human-in-the-loop isn't optional in HR.

AI screens and ranks candidates.
Human reviews recommendations.
Human makes final decision.
Decision rationale is documented.
Patterns are audited for bias.

AI supports decisions. It never makes them.

That's the Fulcrum methodology.

### POST 13: THE AGENTIC SPECTRUM

Not every HR problem needs the same AI approach.

**Level 1: Assistants** (Human-in-the-loop, conversational)
→ Best for: Job descriptions, employee communications, performance feedback
→ Why: You maintain control over every output

**Level 2: Automations** (Trigger-based, workflow)
→ Best for: Interview scheduling, onboarding reminders, benefits enrollment follow-ups
→ Why: Frees you from administrative follow-up

**Level 3: Agents** (Autonomous, multi-step)
→ Best for: Compensation benchmarking, policy research, engagement survey analysis
→ Why: Research-intensive work requiring synthesis across sources

The 80% rule: 80% of HR work benefits from assistants, not agents.

Why? Because HR is about relationships, not just outputs.

## CATEGORY 4: ETHICS, BIAS & COMPLIANCE (10 POSTS)

### POST 36: THE BIAS PROBLEM

Truth: AI inherits bias from training data.

If historical hiring data shows bias toward certain schools, backgrounds, or demographics, AI will learn and replicate that bias.

This is the biggest concern HR professionals have.

And it's valid.

Here's the counterintuitive truth:

AI can actually REDUCE bias when used right.

How?

Human hiring has massive bias problems:
→ Resume screening favors familiar names and schools
→ Interviews favor people who look and sound like interviewers
→ Promotion decisions favor whoever's loudest in the room

AI doesn't eliminate bias.

But it makes bias **visible and auditable** in ways human decisions never are.

You can't fix what you can't measure.

AI makes bias measurable.

The solution: Human-in-the-loop + audit trails.

Never let AI make final HR decisions. Ever.

---

### POST 37: EEOC COMPLIANCE

The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission is watching AI in hiring closely.

What you need to know:

**1. Disparate Impact Rules Still Apply**
If your AI tool produces different outcomes by protected class, you're liable—even if the AI vendor built it.

**2. You Must Be Able to Explain Decisions**
"The AI said so" is not a defense. You need to know why AI recommended or rejected candidates.

**3. Regular Audits Are Essential**
Test your AI tools for disparate impact across protected classes. Quarterly minimum.

**4. Transparency Matters**
Candidates have the right to know if AI is involved in hiring decisions. Disclose it.

This isn't optional.

This is table stakes for responsible AI in HR.

### POST 39: THE FIVE QUESTIONS

Before implementing any HR AI tool, ask:

**1. Can I explain how this AI makes recommendations?**
If not, don't use it.

**2. Can I audit outcomes by protected class?**
If not, you're creating legal risk.

**3. Is there a human checkpoint before action?**
If not, add one.

**4. What happens if the AI is wrong?**
If you don't have a process for this, build one.

**5. Would I be comfortable defending this in court?**
If not, rethink it.

These aren't nice-to-haves.

These are table stakes for responsible AI in HR.

### POST 41: AUDIT TRAILS

"The AI said so" is not a defense.

When using AI in HR decisions, you need audit trails:

**For hiring decisions:**
→ What criteria did AI evaluate?
→ What data did it consider?
→ Why was this candidate ranked higher than others?
→ Who reviewed the AI recommendation?
→ What was the human decision-maker's rationale?

**For promotion decisions:**
→ What performance data informed the AI analysis?
→ What bias checks were performed?
→ How was the recommendation validated?
→ What was the final human decision and why?

Document everything.

If you can't explain your AI-assisted decision process to the EEOC, don't deploy the tool.

### POST 44: DISPARATE IMPACT TESTING

If you're using AI in hiring, you need to test for disparate impact.

Quarterly minimum.

Here's how:

**1. Collect outcome data by protected class:**
→ Application pass-through rates
→ Interview rates
→ Offer rates
→ Hire rates

**2. Compare outcomes across groups:**
→ Are certain demographics advancing at different rates?
→ Is the difference statistically significant?
→ Can you explain the difference with job-related factors?

**3. If disparate impact exists:**
→ Investigate the cause
→ Adjust AI criteria if needed
→ Document your analysis and remediation
→ Consult employment counsel

This isn't optional.

EEOC enforcement is coming for AI hiring tools.

Better to catch and fix disparate impact yourself than wait for a lawsuit.
